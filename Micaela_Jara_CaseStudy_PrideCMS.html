Product Case Study

Testing Custom Fields for Pride.com on the RebelMouse CMS

How systematic QA on a high-profile editorial platform caught critical content rendering failures — and how developer collaboration turned bugs into clearer requirements.

Company RebelMouse
Client Pride.com
Platform RebelMouse CMS
Focus Area Custom Fields · UX · Requirements
01 — Context

A flagship editorial platform with complex content needs

Pride.com is a high-visibility media brand. As a RebelMouse client, their editorial team relies on the CMS to publish content across a range of formats — articles, features, multimedia — each with specific metadata and presentation requirements.

To meet those needs, the platform uses custom fields: configurable data inputs that editors use to control how content is labeled, categorized, and displayed on the frontend. When these fields break, editors lose control of the product — and readers see a broken experience.

💡 Custom fields are invisible infrastructure. When they work, no one notices. When they don't, the entire editorial workflow breaks down.

02 — Problem Discovery

Fields that broke silently or rendered incorrectly

During dedicated QA testing of the custom fields feature in the RebelMouse CMS, I identified two distinct failure patterns affecting Pride.com's editorial workflow:

Bug Type A
Fields breaking under certain conditions

Some custom fields failed to save or behaved unexpectedly depending on the content type, field combination, or order of operations during editing.

Bug Type B
Incorrect rendering on the frontend

Other fields saved correctly in the CMS but didn't display as expected on the published page — wrong output, missing values, or inconsistent presentation.

Both issues created a disconnect between what the editorial team configured in the CMS and what the audience actually saw — undermining trust in the tool and the reliability of the product.

03 — Process

From test cases to requirements clarification

1
Designed systematic test cases for custom fields

I built a structured set of test cases covering each custom field type: different input formats, edge cases, field combinations, and content types. This ensured coverage beyond the happy path.

2
Identified and documented failure patterns

Through repeated testing, I mapped exactly which conditions triggered each bug: which field types broke, under what inputs, and whether the failure was at the save layer or the render layer.

3
Collaborated directly with developers

Rather than just filing tickets, I had active conversations with the dev team to walk through the failures, share reproduction steps, and discuss expected vs actual behavior. This back-and-forth was key to resolving ambiguous cases.

4
Clarified requirements together

Some failures revealed that the requirements themselves were unclear or incomplete. In those conversations, we defined how the fields should behave — turning edge cases into documented, agreed-upon specifications.

5
Validated fixes and confirmed frontend rendering

After each fix, I re-tested both the CMS behavior and the frontend output to verify that fields saved, rendered, and displayed correctly across content types and scenarios.

05 — Product Thinking

What this experience reinforced about product work

This project made visible something that's easy to underestimate: the gap between what's built and what was meant. Several of the bugs I found weren't bugs in the traditional sense — they were symptoms of requirements that had never been fully defined.

Working through those edge cases with developers wasn't just bug-fixing — it was requirement discovery. We were collectively figuring out how the feature should behave, and then encoding that into the product. That process is at the heart of what a Product Manager does: bridging intent and implementation.

🧠 The best QA work is requirements work in disguise. Finding where a feature breaks is often finding where the spec ended — and where product thinking needs to begin.

Product Case Study

Responsive UX Audit for Paper Magazine

Identifying and fixing CSS-level breakpoints failures that degraded the reading experience on mobile and tablet — for one of culture's most iconic digital publications.

Company RebelMouse
Client Paper Magazine
Platform papermag.com
Focus Area Responsive Design · UX · CSS Debugging
01 — Context

A design-forward publication where visual presentation is the product

Paper Magazine is one of the most visually distinctive media brands in culture. Its digital presence is inseparable from its identity — readers expect the same editorial boldness online as in print. For a publication like Paper, a broken layout isn't just a bug. It's a brand failure.

As a RebelMouse client, Paper's site needed to deliver a consistent, polished experience across all screen sizes — from desktop editorial spreads to mobile-first browsing, where the majority of their audience reads.

"For a design-forward brand, a layout that breaks on mobile isn't a minor bug — it's the product failing its audience at the moment they most need it."

02 — Problem Discovery

CSS failures breaking the experience on mobile and tablet

During responsive UX testing across device breakpoints, I identified a range of layout failures affecting both mobile and tablet viewports. The issues stemmed from CSS-level errors — styles that worked correctly on desktop but broke at smaller screen sizes.

📱
Mobile
Layout and element failures

Elements overflowing their containers, incorrect stacking, text truncation, and components misaligned or hidden at small viewports.

📐
Tablet
Intermediate breakpoint gaps

Styles designed for desktop or mobile didn't gracefully adapt to tablet widths, leaving visual inconsistencies at mid-range breakpoints.

The root cause in each case was CSS: missing or incorrect media queries, properties not accounting for smaller viewports, and styles that assumed a fixed desktop width.

03 — Process

Systematic cross-device testing and CSS-level diagnosis

1
Cross-device UX testing across breakpoints

I systematically tested the site across mobile and tablet viewports, navigating core user journeys — homepage, article pages, navigation — at each breakpoint to surface visual and functional failures.

2
Browser DevTools inspection to identify CSS root causes

Using browser DevTools, I inspected the computed styles on broken elements, identified which CSS rules were causing the failures, and pinpointed the exact properties and media queries at fault.

3
Documented bugs with visual references and device specs

Each issue was documented with screenshots, device/viewport details, and the specific CSS property or breakpoint causing the problem — giving developers everything they needed to reproduce and fix efficiently.

4
Collaborated with dev team on fixes

I worked alongside developers to validate proposed CSS fixes, retesting at each affected breakpoint to confirm the layout rendered correctly before sign-off.

5
Full regression pass across devices

After fixes were applied, I ran a full regression test across mobile and tablet to confirm all issues were resolved and no new breakages were introduced.

05 — Product Thinking

What responsive testing taught me about product quality

Responsive design is one of those areas where the gap between "works on my machine" and "works for users" is widest. Developers build on desktops. Users read on phones. Without deliberate cross-device testing, that gap goes undetected until it's live.

Working on Paper's responsive QA reinforced something I carry into product thinking: quality is defined by the worst experience a user can have, not the best. A homepage that looks stunning on a 27-inch monitor and breaks on an iPhone is a broken product — full stop.

Going deeper into CSS also sharpened my ability to have technical conversations with frontend developers — understanding not just that something looks wrong, but why, and what it takes to fix it. That technical fluency makes cross-functional collaboration faster and more precise.

"A PM who can read a broken layout and speak the language of breakpoints can move faster with engineering — and advocate more precisely for the user."
